### Pricing Psychology & Tactics

- **Use cost-plus pricing with psychological pricing** â€” e.g., $3.95 vs $4.00 for beverages (Ben Miller, 1/14/26)
- **Start higher, then adjust down** â€” "Underpricing is harder to recover from" (Jason Carlin, 8/20/25)
- **Cluster pricing by category** for customer simplicity (Kyle Davey, 7/17/25)
  - All chips at one price, all energy drinks at one price, etc.
- **Price to gas-station parity or slightly above** â€” this is the benchmark, NOT Walmart (Lane Folkers, 9/15/25)
- **Apartments tolerate higher pricing** due to convenience factor (Lane Folkers, 10/6/25)
- **Smart stores often don't show shelf prices** â€” users see at POS, reducing price sensitivity (Lane Folkers, 10/6/25)

### Profit Over Margin Percentage

> **Key insight:** "Prioritize profit over percentage marginâ€”larger high-cost items can be more profitable than smaller high-margin items." â€” Anthony (VendHub, 1/13/26)

- A $5 item at 40% margin = $2 profit per unit

- **Evaluate freezer performance at the freezer level, not by individual item** (Weekly Call, 12/17/25)
- "Focus is less on margin and more on pennies earned per transaction" (Charles Wheeler, 9/16/25)

### Bottles vs. Cans Strategy

- **Prefer bottles over cans** for higher dollar profit per unit
- "Avoid cans if you can: bottles boost profit per sale" (Lane Folkers)
- In some markets, operators price cans = bottles (e.g., $4 in LA)
- 16.9oz bottles preferred for better margin and manageable shelf life
- Transition to 20oz when feasible for even better margins
- Cans still needed in some price-sensitive markets â€” can use as "relationship sacrifices"

### White-Collar vs. Residential Pricing

- **White-collar offices** are more price-sensitive â€” surveys show complaints about pricing being "42% higher than Walmart"
- **Apartments** tolerate higher prices due to convenience â€” especially luxury apartments
- **Business locations** may restrict pricing changes and demand detailed price lists
- Consider offering cheaper cans alongside premium bottles at white-collar sites

## 2. Location Strategy

### Best Location Types (Ranked)

1. **High-rise apartment towers** (downtown, with Airbnb units) â€” best for open markets, $6K-7K/month
2. **Luxury apartments** (200+ units, high occupancy) â€” strong and less price-sensitive
3. **Large office buildings** (200+ employees) â€” high foot traffic
4. **Medical buildings** (300+ staff + foot traffic) â€” strong lead
5. **Manufacturing/warehouses** â€” lucrative ($10K+/month) but competitive
6. **Student housing** â€” high volume but watch for theft

### Location Evaluation Criteria

- **Unit count/resident count** â€” primary driver
- **Foot traffic patterns** â€” daily habits matter most
- **Placement within the building** â€” mailroom > clubhouse > fitness center > poolside
- **Occupancy rate** â€” enter early at high-potential properties, even at 0% occupancy
- **Demographics** â€” affects pricing tolerance and product mix
- **Visibility** â€” machines must be easily seen and accessible
- **24/7 access** â€” avoid time-restricted clubhouses

### What Works

- **Mailrooms/package rooms** â€” consistently strongest performers in garden-style apartments
- **Elevator lobbies** â€” forced foot traffic
- **Break rooms in offices** â€” captive audience
- **Downtown tower lobbies** â€” Airbnb guest traffic is a bonus
- **Being "first in" at new properties** â€” builds strong placement relationships

### What Doesn't Work

- **Garden-style clubhouses** â€” limited traffic, theft risk
- **Fitness centers/pool areas** â€” consistently underperform
- **Time-restricted common rooms** â€” hours kill sales
- **Sight from the street** â€” invites vandalism ("Don't put machines in sight from the street" â€” Kyle Davey)
- **Suboptimal placement by property managers** â€” push back diplomatically

## 4. Scaling

### Growth Path

- **Start small** â€” 2 machines at a new location, scale based on performance
- **Build operational "muscle memory"** before scaling
- **Pop-ins are essential** â€” "Stopped doing pop-ins after landing first location â€” big mistake. Momentum matters!" (Thomas Rohlader)
- **Typical process to close a location:** ~3 months

### Hiring Stockers

- **Craigslist** most effective platform â€” screen for vehicle capacity (aim to fit 6+ bins)
- Word-of-mouth via social media also effective
- Start with trusted people in your network
- **Part-time vs. full-time trade-off:** PT = more flexible but higher turnover; FT = stability but risk if they leave
- **Multiple part-time stockers** recommended â€” if one quits, others cover
- **Show up and grind with your team** â€” best morale boost (Anthony Kolodziej)

### Financing Strategy

- **0% APR credit cards** â€” operators funding ~$50K in equipment with staggered cards
- Chase Ink Cash/Unlimited: $6K spend/3 months, $900 bonus, 0% APR for 12 months
- Amex Blue Business Plus: $3K spend, lower bonus, easier approval
- **Stagger applications 30-90 days apart** to extend interest-free timelines
- Track with spreadsheets + Travel Freely app
- **Pay off before promo APR ends** â€” otherwise 18-25% kicks in

### Route Efficiency

- Monitor revenue-to-labor ratios as you scale
- Example benchmark: $3,500 revenue for 8 labor hours + expenses
- **Move underperforming units** before buying more â€” one operator doubled revenue by moving a micro-market to a different building within the same campus

### Scaling Financials

- **Buying existing routes:** 0.5x-1x annual revenue for distressed routes; up to 2x for better-run setups
- PE groups entering the market buying routes >$10M/year â€” "Supply & Demand in an industry going through disruption is great for you guys"
- **Micro-markets consistently outperform** traditional vending and smart coolers, often 50-100% more sales

## 5. Mistakes & Lessons Learned

### Top Mistakes (from Kyle Davey, Thomas Rohlader, and community)

1. **Stopping pop-ins after first location** â€” momentum matters, keep prospecting
2. **Not testing every item before opening** â€” scan everything, SKUs change
3. **Placing machines in sight from the street** â€” invites vandalism
4. **Underpricing** â€” harder to raise prices than to start high
5. **Overcommitting on equipment** before qualifying the location
6. **Not registering as reseller** at Costco/Sam's â€” paying unnecessary sales tax
7. **Ignoring planogram dimensions** â€” tall bottles waste vertical shelf space
8. **Leaving damaged machines in bad neighborhoods** â€” "Bad guys probably aren't going to stop"
9. **Promising "all healthy" product mix** â€” products won't move
10. **Not having backup stockers** â€” "Two stockers quit abruptly via text on the day they were supposed to stock"

### Location-Specific Failures

- **89-unit luxury place with Stockwell:** Only $97 first week despite "good lobby location" â€” "Win some, lose some"
- **Government contract with 15% rev share + insurance:** Only $700/month across machines â€” "Should have scouted locations ahead of time"
- **Open market in garden-style clubhouse:** Failed due to limited traffic and theft
- **Two micro-markets in admin building ($18K equipment):** Less than $100/day â€” moved one to maintenance building, doubled revenue

### Fresh Food Challenges

- "Fresh food â€” I've had a lot of it spoil and go bad. That takes a LOT more management and logistics to make a success. Tread carefully!" (Kyle Davey)
- Fresh items require careful quantity planning
- Minimum $2 profit target on expiring items like sandwiches
- "Better to sell quality items that move than cheap items that sit and expire"

### Technical Mistakes

- **Stockwell lockout:** Machine went into temperature lockout mode after install â€” no sales until manually fixed
- **Connectivity failures:** SIM cards can be spotty; dedicated routers more reliable
- **Unlocking Stockwell with key during power outage:** Door doesn't relock â€” nearly cleaned out of drinks
- **Stripped screws on delivery:** Keep a screw extractor kit handy

### Route Acquisition Strategy (Jason Carlin, 1/7/2026)

- **Buy at 50-80% of annualized revenue** â€” preferred range
