- **Kyle's approach:** Pop-ins 1-2x per week when actively growing

### What to Say / Pitch Elements
1. **Frame it as an amenity, not a vendor placement**

   - Leasing agents use it to show potential tenants
2. **Use the $54 rent premium stat:** Having vending/convenience equates to $54/unit in premium rent â€” that's added NOI for the owner

### Qualifying Questions During Pop-ins
- Where's the most foot traffic in the building?
- How many units/employees? (Get specific per shift)
- Do you supply snacks for free?

- Is the company growing/hiring?

## âš™ï¸ Operations

### Restocking Frequency
- **High-volume locations (unicorn):** 3-4x per week (Shannon's $25K location: Monday, Wednesday, Friday + Saturday/Sunday)
- **Standard locations:** Weekly or as needed
- **Critical lesson:** Shannon's first location sold out in 48 HOURS â€” be prepared to adjust

### Inventory Management
- **Early stage:** You can eyeball it with a few machines
- **At scale (15-25+ machines):** You MUST systemize
  - Kyle: "I walked into the warehouse and the rack where we keep the Coke was full. After one day of restocking, it was almost empty. I'm like, all right, I'm going to have to figure out how to systemize this."
  - Create scheduled sales analysis per machine
  - Track what's selling vs. sitting

### The Variety Pack Problem
> "I would order Gatorade Zero and it would come in a variety pack â€” fruit punch, glacier cherry, lemon lime. Lemon lime sells 3x more. My warehouse guy sends me photos of oversupply of the other flavors." â€” Anthony

- **Effective cost per unit with variety packs is 50+ cents HIGHER** due to waste/oversupply
- **Solution:** Use distributor relationships to order single-SKU cases
- This applies to ANY variety pack from Costco/Sam's

### Sourcing Strategy (Progressive)
1. **Starting out:** Costco Business Center, Sam's Club
2. **Scaling up:** Distributor relationships for single-SKU ordering, better pricing, delivery
3. **Key advantages of distributors:**
   - Single-flavor ordering (no variety pack waste)
   - Rebate checks quarterly (3-12% depending on product)
   - Competitive advantage in sales pitches
   - Delivery to warehouse (saves time)

## âŒ Key Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Placing Machines for Convenience, Not Traffic
> "Don't focus on what's convenient for you. Focus on high foot traffic." â€” Anthony

Anthony's $700/month machine was 5 minutes from his house. His $4,000-5,000/month machines are in properly qualified locations.

### 2. Not Asking Enough Qualifying Questions
- Don't assume shift sizes â€” ASK specifically
- Don't skip the "do you provide free snacks" question
- Don't forget to check for nearby convenience stores

### 3. Trying to Do Everything Yourself Too Long
> "You can't do $25/hour tasks all the time." â€” Mike to Anthony early on

- Hire before you think you're ready
- The cost of not doing pop-ins (because you're stocking) far exceeds the cost of a stocker

### 4. Paying Cash for Machines Instead of Financing
- Conserve cash for operations, hiring, and inventory
- Finance the machines â€” the revenue covers payments

### 5. Buying Variety Packs at Scale
- The waste from unsold flavors makes your effective cost per unit much higher
- Get distributor relationships for single-SKU ordering ASAP

### 6. Only Having One Employee
- When Kyle's only employee quit on the spot (dropped keys at the door), everything fell apart
- Always have backup: 2+ part-timers so one leaving doesn't kill operations

### 7. Not Having a CRM
- With a long sales cycle (10-12 weeks to 6-8 months), you WILL lose leads without one
- Track every pop-in, every follow-up, every contact

### 8. Getting Discouraged by Rejection
- It's a numbers game â€” 1 in 10 is normal
- The sales cycle is long â€” results come in waves
- "If you're not out there hustling to get it, somebody else is going to." â€” Kyle

### 9. Ignoring Competition (or Letting It Intimidate You)
- Kyle had a competitor send him a cease-and-desist letter to try to bully him out
- His response: "Let's go. Game on, buddy." â€” He went harder
- Don't worry about googling competitors â€” focus on your own execution

### 10. Not Asking for Referrals
- Every property manager knows other property managers
